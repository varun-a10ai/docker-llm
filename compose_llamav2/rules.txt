1. **Subject-verb agreement errors** - The subject and verb of a sentence must agree in number.
   "She walk to the store every day." => "She walks to the store every day."

2. **Wrong tense used** - Verb tense should be consistent throughout a sentence.
   "I go to the cinema yesterday." => "I went to the cinema yesterday."

3. **Double negatives** - Using two negatives in a sentence can turn the meaning into a positive.
   "I don't need no help." => "I don't need any help."

4. **Misplaced modifiers** - A modifier must be placed next to the word it is supposed to modify.
   "She found a gold man's watch." => "She found a man's gold watch."

5. **Incorrect pronoun reference** - Pronouns must clearly refer to specific nouns.
   "Jake told his brother that he failed." => "Jake told his brother that he had failed."

6. **Wrong word form** - Using the wrong form of a word.
   "I must to go." => "I must go."

7. **Wrong preposition** - Incorrect use of a preposition.
   "I am good in math." => "I am good at math."

8. **Fragment sentences** - Sentences must have a subject and a predicate.
   "Walking down the street." => "I was walking down the street."

9. **Run-on sentences** - Two independent clauses need to be separated by punctuation or a conjunction.
   "I love to read I have many books." => "I love to read. I have many books."

10. **Dangling participles** - The noun that the participle modifies should be clearly stated.
    "Walking through the door, the view was breathtaking." => "Walking through the door, I found the view breathtaking."

11. **Incorrect comparative or superlative form** - The wrong form of comparison is used.
    "She is more taller than me." => "She is taller than me."

12. **Inappropriate shift in verb tense** - Verb tenses should remain consistent within a sentence.
    "I won the lottery and buy a house." => "I won the lottery and bought a house."

13. **Incorrect article usage** - Wrong use or omission of a/an and the.
    "I saw the eagle." (when it's not a specific eagle) => "I saw an eagle."

14. **Inconsistent parallel structure** - When making a list or comparison, the sentence elements should match in type.
    "He likes running, to swim, and hiking." => "He likes running, swimming, and hiking."

15. **Incorrect use of infinitives** - Infinitives should not be used where base forms are required and vice versa.
    "She helped her mother to cook." => "She helped her mother cook."

16. **Incorrect use of reflexive pronouns** - Reflexive pronouns refer back to the subject of the clause.
    "My sister and myself went shopping." => "My sister and I went shopping."

17. **Incorrect use of adverbs** - Adverbs modify verbs, not adjectives or other adverbs.
    "She drives very careful." => "She drives very carefully."

18. **Incorrect capitalization** - Proper nouns and the first word of a sentence should be capitalized.
    "james lives in new york." => "James lives in New York."

19. **Incorrect subject pronoun** - Subject pronouns should be used as subjects.
    "Me and my friends went to a party." => "My friends and I went to a party."

20. **Incorrect object pronoun** - Object pronouns should be used as objects.
    "She gave it to I." => "She gave it to me."

21. **Incorrect possessive pronoun** - Possessive pronouns should show possession.
    "Is that car your's?" => "Is that car yours?"

22. **Unnecessary use of passive voice** - Active voice should be used unless the doer is unknown or unimportant.
    "The ball was thrown by John." => "John threw the ball."

23. **Incorrect use of apostrophes** - Apostrophes show possession or contraction, not plurals.
    "The dog's are in the yard." => "The dogs are in the yard."

24. **Misuse of homophones** - Words that sound the same but have different meanings.
    "I went to the see." => "I went to the sea."

25. **Incorrect use of conjunctions** - Conjunctions join clauses, sentences, or words.
    "I like tea but not I don't like coffee." => "I like tea but I don't like coffee."

26. **Incorrect word order** - Words in a sentence should be in the correct order.
    "The cat the chased dog." => "The cat chased the dog."

27. **Wrong use of conditionals** - Incorrect use of conditional forms (if, unless, etc.)
    "If I would have known, I wouldn't have come." => "If I had known, I wouldn't have come."

28. **Incorrect verb form** - Using the wrong form of the verb.
    "She don't like fish." => "She doesn't like fish."

29. **Confusing 'its' and 'it's'** - 'Its' is a possessive pronoun and 'it's' is a contraction of 'it is'.
    "Its raining outside." => "It's raining outside."

30. **Confusing 'your' and 'you're'** - 'Your' is a possessive pronoun and 'you're' is a contraction of 'you are'.
    "Your welcome." => "You're welcome."

31. **Misplaced or missing commas** - Commas should be used correctly to separate elements in a sentence.
    "I enjoy cooking my family and my pets." => "I enjoy cooking, my family, and my pets."

32. **Wrong use of the infinitive** - Incorrect use of 'to' with verbs.
    "I must to leave." => "I must leave."

33. **Confusing 'there', 'their', and 'they're'** - 'There' indicates a place, 'their' is a possessive pronoun, and 'they're' is a contraction of 'they are'.
    "Their is a cat in they're house over there." => "There is a cat in their house over there."

34. **Using 'literally' in a non-literal context** - 'Literally' should mean 'exactly' or 'actually'.
    "I was literally dying of laughter." => "I was practically dying of laughter."

35. **Confusing 'less' and 'fewer'** - 'Less' refers to singular mass nouns, 'fewer' to plural count nouns.
    "There are less cars on the road." => "There are fewer cars on the road."

36. **Redundancy** - Saying the same thing more than once.
    "Return back to your seats." => "Return to your seats."

37. **Incorrect agreement with collective nouns** - Collective nouns can be singular or plural, depending on context.
    "The team are celebrating." (When

 referring to the team as a collection of individuals) => "The team is celebrating."

38. **Incorrect use of 'me' and 'I'** - 'Me' is an object pronoun, 'I' is a subject pronoun.
    "He gave the books to my friend and I." => "He gave the books to my friend and me."

39. **Confusing 'lay' and 'lie'** - 'Lay' requires a direct object, 'lie' does not.
    "I'm going to lay down." => "I'm going to lie down."

40. **Incorrect use of 'who' and 'whom'** - 'Who' is a subject pronoun, 'whom' is an object pronoun.
    "Whom did you say that was?" => "Who did you say that was?"

41. **Confusing 'which' and 'that' in relative clauses** - 'That' is used in restrictive clauses, 'which' in non-restrictive (often set off by commas).
    "I read the book that was on the table." (The book was on the table, not another) => "I read the book, which was on the table." (The book just happened to be on the table)

42. **Confusing 'i.e.' and 'e.g.'** - 'i.e.' means 'that is,' 'e.g.' means 'for example'.
    "I love fruit, e.g., bananas." (It seems like bananas are the only fruit you love) => "I love fruit, i.e., bananas." (You love fruit, in other words, bananas)

43. **Split infinitives** - Avoid placing an adverb between 'to' and a verb.
    "She decided to quickly leave." => "She decided to leave quickly."

44. **Incorrect use of 'like' and 'as'** - 'Like' is a preposition, 'as' is a conjunction.
    "She sings like an angel does." => "She sings as an angel does."

45. **Incorrect use of 'since' and 'because'** - 'Since' refers to time, 'because' refers to causation.
    "I went to the store because I was out of milk." (It seems like you went to the store at the time you were out of milk) => "I went to the store since I was out of milk." (You went to the store because you were out of milk)

46. **Confusing 'then' and 'than'** - 'Then' refers to time, 'than' is used in comparisons.
    "I am taller then you." => "I am taller than you."

47. **Incorrect use of 'affect' and 'effect'** - 'Affect' is usually a verb meaning 'to influence,' 'effect' is usually a noun meaning 'result'.
    "The medicine had a strong effect on me." (The medicine influenced you) => "The medicine had a strong affect on me." (The medicine had a strong result on you)

48. **Confusing 'farther' and 'further'** - 'Farther' refers to physical distance, 'further' refers to an extension of time or degree.
    "She ran further than me." (She ran a greater distance than you) => "She ran farther than me." (She ran more extended in time or degree than you)

49. **Confusing 'historic' and 'historical'** - 'Historic' means 'important in history,' 'historical' means 'concerning history.'
    "We visited a historical site." (The site is important in history) => "We visited a historic site." (The site is concerning history)

50. **Incorrect use of 'comprise' and 'compose'** - 'Comprise' means 'to include,' 'compose' means 'to make up.'
    "The USA is comprised of 50 states." (The USA includes 50 states) => "The USA is composed of 50 states." (50 states make up the USA)

51. **Incorrect use of 'loose' and 'lose'** - 'Loose' is the opposite of tight, 'lose' is the opposite of win.
    "I hope I don't loose the game." => "I hope I don't lose the game."

52. **Incorrect use of 'envelop' and 'envelope'** - 'Envelop' means to cover or surround, 'envelope' is a container for a letter.
    "Please place the letter in the envelop." => "Please place the letter in the envelope."

53. **Incorrect use of 'insure', 'ensure', and 'assure'** - 'Insure' relates to insurance, 'ensure' means make certain, 'assure' means tell someone something positively.
    "I want to insure that you understand." => "I want to ensure that you understand."

54. **Incorrect use of 'continual' and 'continuous'** - 'Continual' means happening repeatedly with breaks in between, 'continuous' means without interruption.
    "The noise was continual." (The noise was without interruption) => "The noise was continuous." (The noise was happening repeatedly with breaks)

55. **Incorrect use of 'capital' and 'capitol'** - 'Capital' refers to a city, money, or uppercase letters, 'capitol' is a building where legislative work is done.
    "The capitol of France is Paris." => "The capital of France is Paris."

56. **Incorrect use of 'accept' and 'except'** - 'Accept' means to receive, 'except' means excluding.
    "I'll except all the presents." => "I'll accept all the presents."

57. **Incorrect use of 'already' and 'all ready'** - 'Already' refers to time, 'all ready' means completely prepared.
    "I'm all ready finished with my homework." => "I'm already finished with my homework."

58. **Incorrect use of 'desert' and 'dessert'** - 'Desert' is a dry, barren area of land, 'dessert' is the sweet course eaten at the end of a meal.
    "I would like some desert." => "I would like some dessert."

59. **Incorrect use of 'principle' and 'principal'** - 'Principle' is a basic truth or law, 'principal' is a person who has controlling authority.
    "The principle of the school is very strict." => "The principal of the school is very strict."

60. **Confusing 'everyday' and 'every day'** - 'Everyday' is an adjective meaning commonplace, 'every day' means each day.
    "I go to the gym everyday." => "I go to the gym every day."

61. **Incorrect use of 'cite', 'site', and 'sight'** - 'Cite' means to reference, 'site' is a location, 'sight' is the ability to see.
    "The sight of the new building is beautiful." => "The site of the new building is beautiful."

62. **Confusing 'compliment' and 'complement'** - 'Compliment' is a nice thing you say about someone, 'complement' is something

 that completes or goes well with something.
    "The tie compliments his suit." => "The tie complements his suit."

63. **Incorrect use of 'fewer' and 'less'** - 'Fewer' is for items you can count, 'less' is for singular mass nouns.
    "I have less apples than you." => "I have fewer apples than you."

64. **Confusing 'stationary' and 'stationery'** - 'Stationary' means not moving, 'stationery' refers to paper, pens, and other office items.
    "I wrote a letter on my stationary." => "I wrote a letter on my stationery."

65. **Confusing 'disinterested' and 'uninterested'** - 'Disinterested' means impartial, 'uninterested' means not interested.
    "I'm uninterested in the outcome of the game." (I don't care about the game at all) => "I'm disinterested in the outcome of the game." (I don't have a stake in the game)

66. **Confusing 'breath' and 'breathe'** - 'Breath' is a noun, the air taken into or expelled from the lungs, 'breathe' is a verb, to inhale and exhale.
    "I need to take a breathe." => "I need to take a breath."

67. **Incorrect use of 'former' and 'latter'** - 'Former' refers to the first of two things, 'latter' refers to the second.
    "Of coffee and tea, I prefer the latter." (I prefer coffee) => "Of coffee and tea, I prefer the former." (I prefer coffee)

68. **Incorrect use of 'elicit' and 'illicit'** - 'Elicit' means to draw out, 'illicit' means illegal.
    "His behavior was designed to illicit a response." => "His behavior was designed to elicit a response."

69. **Confusing 'passed' and 'past'** - 'Passed' is the past tense of 'pass,' 'past' refers to the time gone by.
    "I walked passed the park." => "I walked past the park."

70. **Incorrect use of 'precede' and 'proceed'** - 'Precede' means to come before, 'proceed' means to go ahead.
    "We will proceed the meeting with lunch." => "We will precede the meeting with lunch."

71. **Confusing 'allusion' and 'illusion'** - 'Allusion' is an indirect reference, 'illusion' is a false idea or belief.
    "He made an illusion to the previous agreement." => "He made an allusion to the previous agreement."

72. **Incorrect use of 'born' and 'borne'** - 'Born' is always the past participle of the verb 'bear' when it refers to birth, 'borne' is used in all other senses.
    "She has borne two children." (She has given birth to two children) => "She has born two children." (She has given birth to two children)

73. **Incorrect use of 'fair' and 'fare'** - 'Fair' can be an adjective meaning just or beautiful, a noun meaning an exhibition, or an adverb meaning clearly, 'fare' refers to a fee or food.
    "The fair for the bus ride is $2." => "The fare for the bus ride is $2."

74. **Incorrect use of 'lead' and 'led'** - 'Lead' can be a noun meaning a type of metal or a verb meaning to guide, 'led' is the past tense of the verb 'lead.'
    "She lead the team last year." => "She led the team last year."

75. **Incorrect use of 'pedal' and 'peddle'** - 'Pedal' is a foot-operated lever, 'peddle' means to sell.
    "He pedaled his wares at the market." => "He peddled his wares at the market."

76. **Incorrect use of 'pour' and 'pore'** - 'Pour' means to flow rapidly, 'pore' as a verb means to read or study attentively.
    "I'm going to pour over these documents tonight." => "I'm going to pore over these documents tonight."

77. **Incorrect use of 'peek', 'peak', and 'pique'** - 'Peek' means to look quickly, 'peak' is the top point, and 'pique' means to stimulate interest.
    "My interest is peaked." => "My interest is piqued."

78. **Confusing 'loathe' and 'loath'** - 'Loathe' means to dislike greatly, 'loath' means reluctant.
    "I'm loathe to admit that I made a mistake." => "I'm loath to admit that I made a mistake."

79. **Incorrect use of 'whether' and 'weather'** - 'Whether' introduces alternatives, 'weather' refers to the state of the atmosphere.
    "I don't know weather I'm coming or going." => "I don't know whether I'm coming or going."

80. **Incorrect use of 'canvas' and 'canvass'** - 'Canvas' is a type of fabric, 'canvass' means to seek people's votes.
    "She decided to canvas the neighborhood for support." => "She decided to canvass the neighborhood for support."

81. **Confusing 'bear' and 'bare'** - 'Bear' means to carry, endure or give birth to, 'bare' means naked or to reveal.
    "I can't bare the weight." => "I can't bear the weight."

82. **Confusing 'eminent', 'imminent', and 'immanent'** - 'Eminent' means famous and respected, 'imminent' means about to happen, and 'immanent' means inherent.
    "An immanent scientist predicted the eminent arrival of a comet." => "An eminent scientist predicted the imminent arrival of a comet."

83. **Incorrect use of 'flaunt' and 'flout'** - 'Flaunt' means to show off, 'flout' means to openly disregard.
    "He flouted his wealth." => "He flaunted his wealth."

84. **Incorrect use of 'moot'** - 'Moot' means subject to debate, dispute, or uncertainty, and probably not admitting of a final decision.
    "It's a mute point." => "It's a moot point."

85. **Incorrect use of 'proscribe' and 'prescribe'** - 'Proscribe' means to forbid, 'prescribe' means to recommend.
    "Doctors proscribed medication to treat the illness." => "Doctors prescribed medication to treat the illness."

86. **Incorrect use of 'imply' and 'infer'** - 'Imply' means to suggest, 'infer' means to deduce.
    "Are you inferring that I'm wrong?" => "Are you implying that I'm wrong?"

87. **Incorrect use of 'alright' and 'all right'** - 'Alright' is nonstandard, 'all right' is standard English.
    "Is everything alright

?" => "Is everything all right?"

88. **Incorrect use of 'discreet' and 'discrete'** - 'Discreet' means careful and circumspect, 'discrete' means separate and distinct.
    "We offer a discrete service." => "We offer a discreet service."

89. **Incorrect use of 'capital' and 'capitol'** - 'Capital' refers to a city, wealth, or an uppercase letter, 'capitol' refers to a building where legislative work is done.
    "The capitol of France is Paris." => "The capital of France is Paris."

90. **Incorrect use of 'principle' and 'principal'** - 'Principle' is a fundamental truth or proposition, 'principal' is the most important, consequential, or influential.
    "He's a man of great principal." => "He's a man of great principle."

91. **Confusing 'forth' and 'fourth'** - 'Forth' means forward in time, place, or order; 'fourth' refers to the number 4 in a series.
    "On the forth day of Christmas..." => "On the fourth day of Christmas..."

92. **Incorrect use of 'lose' and 'loose'** - 'Lose' means to be deprived of or cease to have or retain, 'loose' means not firmly or tightly fixed in place.
    "I always loose my keys." => "I always lose my keys."

93. **Confusing 'affect' and 'effect'** - 'Affect' is usually a verb meaning to influence, 'effect' is usually a noun meaning a result.
    "The drug had an immediate affect on her symptoms." => "The drug had an immediate effect on her symptoms."

94. **Incorrect use of 'accept' and 'except'** - 'Accept' means to receive, 'except' means excluding.
    "I like all vegetables accept broccoli." => "I like all vegetables except broccoli."

95. **Incorrect use of 'they're', 'their', and 'there'** - 'They're' is a contraction of 'they are', 'their' is a possessive pronoun, and 'there' refers to a place.
    "Their going to they're house over there." => "They're going to their house over there."

96. **Incorrect use of 'its' and 'it's'** - 'Its' is a possessive pronoun, 'it's' is a contraction of 'it is' or 'it has'.
    "It's tail is long." => "Its tail is long."

97. **Incorrect use of 'you're' and 'your'** - 'You're' is a contraction of 'you are', 'your' is a possessive pronoun.
    "Your going to love it." => "You're going to love it."

98. **Incorrect use of 'to', 'two', and 'too'** - 'To' is a preposition, 'two' is a number, and 'too' means also or excessively.
    "Two much of anything is bad for you." => "Too much of anything is bad for you."

99. **Incorrect use of 'whos' and 'whose'** - 'Whos' is not a word, 'whose' is a possessive pronoun.
    "Whos book is this?" => "Whose book is this?"

100. **Incorrect use of 'then' and 'than'** - 'Then' refers to a time, 'than' is used for comparisons.
    "I'm taller then you." => "I'm taller than you."
